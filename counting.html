The lesson

Japanese counting is one system, three layers: the core numbers (いち、に、さん…), a build rule for everything up to 9,999, and a small set of counters that say what kind of thing you're counting.

The core ten 0–10

Three numbers have two readings. In everyday speech, prefer yon, nana, and kyuu — the alternatives show up in fixed phrases (like months and times).

Counting things ひとつ、ふたつ…

For counting objects, Japanese switches to a second, native set. It works for almost anything — food, drinks, ideas — which makes it the beginner's best friend. It only goes up to ten.

Small objects 〜こ

The counter 〜こ (ko) counts small, roundish things — onigiri, apples, eggs, potions in a game. It uses the core numbers, with a few squeezed sounds.

いっこ or ひとつ? For "+1 potion" both work: ポーション を いっこ (pooshon o ikko) or ポーション を ひとつ (pooshon o hitotsu). When unsure of the right counter, ひとつ・ふたつ… is always a safe fallback — Japanese speakers do this too.

Using a number in a sentence

The amount goes after the thing, right before the verb. This is how you order at a restaurant.

おにぎり を ふたつ ください。

onigiri o futatsu kudasai. — Two onigiri, please.

りんご を みっつ ください。ringo o mittsu kudasai.Three apples, please.
たまご を ろっこ ください。tamago o rokko kudasai.Six eggs, please.
ふたり です。futari desu.(A table for) two, please. — at a restaurant door
