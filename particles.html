に: location, time, target

に marks a fixed point: where something exists, when something happens, or where someone goes/arrives.

1) Location with statement verbs

Use に with existence and residence verbs: いる (living things exist), ある (things exist), and すむ (live/reside).

ここに 猫がいます。

koko ni neko ga imasu. — “There is a cat here.”

ここhere にat/in 猫cat がsubject いますexists
For existence, the place usually comes first: place + に + thing/person + が + います/あります.
テーブルの下に亀がいます。teeburu no shita ni kame ga imasu.There is a turtle under the table.
東京に住んでいます。toukyou ni sundeimasu.I live in Tokyo.
机の上に本があります。tsukue no ue ni hon ga arimasu.There is a book on the desk.

2) Time: at / on

Use に for a specific clock time or calendar day.

九時に起きます。ku ji ni okimasu.I wake up at 9.
金曜日に会います。kinyoubi ni aimasu.I will meet on Friday.
三月に行きます。san gatsu ni ikimasu.I will go in March.
Watch it: relative time words like きょう today, あした tomorrow, and まいにち every day usually do not take に.

3) Destination or purpose

With motion verbs, に can mark the destination or target you reach. It can also mark purpose: “go to do ___.”

学校に行きます。gakkou ni ikimasu.I go to school.
家に帰ります。ie ni kaerimasu.I return home.
映画を見に行きます。eiga o mi ni ikimasu.I go to watch a movie.

の: possession and description

の is pronounced “no.” It connects one noun to another. The first noun modifies the second noun.

私の かばんです。

watashi no kaban desu. — “It is my bag.”

私I / me のmy かばんbag ですis
A の B = B connected to A. Often “A's B,” but not always possession.
PatternMeaning
私のwatashi nomy / mine
私のかばんwatashi no kabanmy bag
これは私のです。kore wa watashi no desu.This is mine.
日本語の先生nihongo no senseiJapanese-language teacher / Japanese teacher
友達の名前はさくらです。tomodachi no namae wa sakura desu.My friend's name is Sakura.
日本の車が好きです。nihon no kuruma ga suki desu.I like Japanese cars.
Quick test: if English can say “A's B,” “B of A,” or “A-type B,” Japanese often uses A の B.
向

へ: direction toward

へ is written へ but pronounced e when it is a particle. It marks the direction you are heading.

京都へ行きます。kyouto e ikimasu.I am going to Kyoto.
日本へようこそ！nihon e youkoso!Welcome to Japan!
学校へ行きます。gakkou e ikimasu.I head to school.

に vs へ with motion

ParticleFeelExample
にdestination / arrival point学校に行きます。I go to school.
へdirection / heading toward学校へ行きます。I head toward school.
For beginner sentences with 行きます, 来ます, and 帰ります, both に and へ can often translate as “to.” The nuance is target vs direction.
